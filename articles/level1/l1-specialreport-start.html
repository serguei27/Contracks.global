An Idea Is Worth Nothing.

Written and narrated by Serguei Poppeleer.

The pitch lands. The room leans in. Someone says “this is really interesting” — and means it. Then the follow-up never comes. Not a no. Just quiet.

Every founder has lived that silence. And almost every founder reads it wrong — replaying the deck for weeks, convinced the story did not land. It landed perfectly. The story was never the problem. The vision excited them. The numbers behind it did not exist yet. And “interesting” is the word people use in the gap between the two.

The room stays excited. The runway does not.

Here is the part nobody says out loud: the idea you are guarding so carefully — the one you make people sign an NDA before you’ll explain — is the part nobody is trying to steal. The same idea is sitting in a dozen other heads in a dozen other cities right now. What is rare, what capital actually pays for, is the part most founders treat as an afterthought. This report is for the founder who has an idea they believe in, and is tired of hearing “interesting” when they need to hear “send the terms.”

Why ideas don’t get funded

Why Exciting Ideas Don’t Get Funded

Most founders believe the same thing: a strong enough idea will attract the capital to build it. Get the vision right, make people feel it, and the money follows. It does not. Vision opens the meeting. It does not close the round.

Investors do not back ideas. They back models that make the idea inevitable.

The data is blunt about where ventures actually die. Startup post-mortem data repeatedly shows the same pattern: ventures fail because the market does not pay, the cash does not last, or the unit economics do not hold. Almost none fail because the idea was bad.

That is the Viability Triangle. Not branding — autopsy data turned into a founder’s filter.

The big misconception

The idea is the hard part — the model comes later. That single belief keeps more good ideas small than any competitor ever will. The idea is the easy part. Ideas are abundant and free, which is exactly why they carry no premium. The model is the scarce thing, and therefore the thing that gets funded.

Mistake 1 — Polishing the deck instead of proving the market

A sharper deck feels like progress. It changes nothing about whether the market will pay. You can perfect the story for months and still have no model underneath it. A deck persuades a room for an hour; a model survives the diligence that follows.

Mistake 2 — Building before anyone agrees to pay

Building feels productive. Features shipped before demand is proven burn the scarcest thing a venture owns — runway — proving the wrong thing. The most expensive way to discover nobody will pay is to build the whole thing first.

Mistake 3 — Keeping the model in your head

When the model lives only in the founder’s head, it is un-backable by anyone who wasn’t in the room. Diligence stalls, the round drifts, and “interesting” becomes the polite no that protects the investor’s optimism about you.

The story gets sharper while the model stays missing.

The Viability Triangle

The Viability Triangle

Most ventures don’t fail at the idea. They fail at the model. Frontier opportunities just expose it faster.

The Viability Triangle is the architecture that turns an idea into an investable business. Three tests decide it — the same three that quietly kill the ventures that fail. Pass all three and you have a model. Fail one and you have a story.

Market — a market that pays

Not a market that admires. A market that pays. A hundred people saying “I’d use that” is enthusiasm; one signed letter of intent, one offtake agreement, one customer who pre-pays is demand. The test is whether money is contracted, not whether interest is expressed.

Money — money that lasts

Ventures die one milestone short of the proof that would have raised the next round. The test is whether the runway reaches the milestone that unlocks capital — not a vague horizon.

Margin — a margin that holds

A margin that only works in the spreadsheet is one you will defend in front of an investor who has seen a thousand of them. The test is whether the unit economics survive real costs — the bad day, not the best case.

Structure — the frame

Market, money and margin only become backable when they are written into a structure someone outside your head can recognise: governance, a defensible commercial case, decisions an outsider can follow. Structure is the difference between an idea and an asset.

Market. Money. Margin. — proof, not theory

Proof · Bleriot — Sustainable Aviation Fuel · Kenya

Sustainable aviation fuel in East Africa was an obvious idea long before it was a company. Abundant feedstock. Demand written into law through CORSIA, ISCC and EU RED II. A continent underserved. None of that was scarce — anyone could see it. What turned it into something backable was the model: an offtake position built against mandated airline demand, a modular micro-refinery design that staged the capital so each increment bought a milestone, and unit economics anchored in real vegetable-oil feedstock rather than a single fragile mega-asset. Built from an idea into an investable SAF platform, with investor interest and scale-up discussions underway. The opportunity was always free. The model was the work.

Proof · The pattern across 30 countries

A sustainable-fuel venture in Kenya and a €2B capital programme in Europe have nothing in common except this: in both, the thing that got backed was never the idea. The idea was the free part everyone could see. What separated the funded from the forgotten was a model that survived being checked.

The idea makes the meeting interesting. The model makes the wire transfer happen.

Five moves

Five Moves to Make It Investable

Move 1 — Prove the market will pay, not that it approves

Stop treating secrecy as strategy. Spend the energy on securing one piece of contracted demand — a letter of intent, a deposit, a pre-paid pilot. Find the difference between admiration and demand before you fund it, not after.

Move 2 — Map the runway to the milestone, not the calendar

Identify the single proof point that unlocks the next tranche of capital, and check the money reaches it with room to spare.

Move 3 — Build the margin on real costs

Construct the unit economics from what things cost when they go wrong, then stress them. A margin that only holds on the best-case line is a wish, not a model.

Move 4 — Write the model into a structure

Move it out of your head and into governance, a clear commercial case, and decisions an outsider can follow. It has to be backable by people who were not in the room when you fell in love with it.

Move 5 — Name what you’ve assumed versus what you’ve tested

An assumption written down is a managed risk. An assumption left unspoken is a future failure with a date on it you have not seen yet. The first hard question in diligence finds every one you skipped.

An investable business is not a better-told idea. It is an idea that survives being checked.

Objections

The Objections You’re Probably Raising

“The idea is the hard part — the model comes later.”

The idea is the easy part; ideas are abundant and free, which is exactly why they get no premium. The model is the scarce thing, and therefore the thing that gets funded. “Later” is usually after the runway has been spent building features nobody agreed to pay for. The model does not come after traction — it is how you earn traction worth having.

“We’ll figure out the numbers once we have customers.”

By then the numbers are no longer a question you answer — they are a verdict you receive. Working out the unit economics before you scale is cheap. Discovering them after you have scaled a business that loses money on every sale is the most expensive lesson in the venture’s life.

“Investors back vision, not spreadsheets.”

They back vision underwritten by a model. Vision is the reason they take the meeting; the model is the reason they wire the money. The rare founder who can hold both is the one who hears “send the terms” instead of “really interesting.”

“If I share the idea, someone will steal it.”

If the idea is that easy to steal, it was never the moat. What is hard to copy is a proven market, a funded path and a margin that holds — and you cannot build any of those in secret. Sharing the idea is how you start testing it.

The core truth

The Core Truth

The idea is the cheapest thing you own. The model is the only thing worth protecting.

Across 30 countries, and ventures and programmes up to €2B, the ones that got backed were almost never the most exciting in the room. They were the most structured. The market was proven, the money was mapped, the margin was real — so when the scrutiny came, there was a business to defend rather than a story to oversell.

The investability ladder — where does your venture sit?

Level 1 — Pitch-Deck Hope. An idea and a deck. No evidence anyone will pay. Story-led.

Level 2 — Build Without Buyers. Product or features underway before any contracted demand exists.

Level 3 — Numbers in the Head. Market, money and margin live in the founder’s head — untested, unwritten.

Level 4 — Tested Model. Demand evidenced, runway mapped to a milestone, unit economics stressed.

Level 5 — Investable Platform. All three tests passed and written into a governed structure. No surprises in diligence.

Growth that is earned looks like this: an investor asks for your model, and it already exists. The round closes on the maths, not the mood in the room. The venture is backable by people who weren’t there when you fell in love with it. And “interesting” stops being the last thing you hear before the silence.

An idea is worth nothing. A model is worth backing.
