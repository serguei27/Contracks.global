The FIDIC Stall That Needed a Reframe — Not a Letter.

Written and narrated by Serguei Poppeleer.

Four hundred million euros of energy infrastructure. The negotiation had stopped dead. Both sides were drafting letters. Everyone was right, and nothing was moving.

Months of correspondence. A binder of letters. Not one metre of pipe in the ground.

Each letter was more precise than the last, each one citing the clause that proved the other side wrong. Legal on both sides was busy. The project was not. Steel sat. The schedule bled a number nobody wanted to say out loud — and every week of stall compounded it.

The pressure in the room ran one direction: escalate. Send the harder letter. Trigger the clause. Get the determination. Be proven right, on the record, in front of everyone.

Here’s the trap. On a FIDIC dispute you can be completely correct and still lose — because being correct doesn’t pour concrete.
Contracks LawThe best claim is the one you never have to write.

The letter that wins the argument doesn’t move the steel. Both sides had built a flawless case for a project that was going nowhere.

The reframe

When I looked at it, the problem wasn’t legal at all. Two parties were arguing positions; neither was talking about the project. Every letter answered who is right? Nobody was answering what is this worth to each of us for every week it stands still?

So we stopped negotiating the dispute and started negotiating the outcome. What does each side genuinely need in order to move? And what does the project lose, in hard terms, every week they don’t?

The stall broke. Not with a better letter. With a better question.

€400M energy infrastructure — FIDIC deadlock, reframed

What happened: two parties, each with a defensible legal position, each escalating in writing while the asset sat idle and the schedule eroded.

Why everyone thought it was rational: both believed they were negotiating. Neither was. They were discussing fault. The project was discussing delay — and only the project was keeping score.

Why the reframe worked: shifted from fault to the shared cost of standstill, the works restarted within weeks. The asset moved toward operation without a determination, and the multi-year claim that had been forming was never filed.

The distance between reporting and control is where claims are born.

How to break a deadlock without a determination

1
Price the standstill, for both sides.

Put a number on what every week of stall costs each party. A shared cost of inaction is the only fact both sides already agree on.

2
Separate being right from getting built.

The clause that proves your case and the move that restarts the works are rarely the same action. Decide which one you actually need this month.

3
Negotiate the outcome, not the dispute.

Ask what each side needs to move, not who was at fault. The first question has an answer everyone can act on. The second has only a winner, a loser — and a stalled project.

The objections this raises

“If we don’t defend our position, we lose the claim.”

Defending the position and moving the project are not mutually exclusive — but treating the letter as the whole strategy makes them so. You can preserve the record and still reframe the conversation. Most stalls break when someone does both.

“The other side started it — why should we move first?”

Because the standstill is charging you both rent every week, regardless of who started it. The party that reframes first isn’t conceding. It’s the one that stops paying for the deadlock soonest.

AI Reality Check

AI can model the cost of standstill quickly — quantifying what each week of delay does to schedule, financing and downstream commitments, which is exactly the number that reframes a stalled dispute.

What it cannot do is have the conversation. The reframe is a human act of judgement: choosing to negotiate the outcome instead of the fault. AI sharpens the case for it. It does not make the call.

The letter defends your position. The reframe moves the project.

The line worth keeping

Most commercial deadlocks aren’t legal problems wearing legal clothes. They’re communication failures — two parties communicating perfectly, each about a different version of the truth.

The letter defends your position. The reframe moves the project.

Only one of them ever gets it built.
