A Decision Without a Deadline Is Just a Delay

The third rail. A RAID log full of open items is not control. It is a list of decisions you have agreed to make later — usually too late.

Written and narrated by Serguei Poppeleer  ·  Founder, Contracks Global  ·  contracks.global

A Decision Without a Deadline Is Just a Delay.

Written and narrated by Serguei Poppeleer.

Open a typical RAID log and you will find dozens of items, carefully recorded, regularly reviewed, and quietly going nowhere. Recording an issue feels like managing it. It is not.

A logged decision with no owner, no date and no consequence is not being managed. It is being watched as it drifts.

The third failure mode is the open item that never closes: a risk noted, an issue raised, a dependency flagged — and then nothing, because no one owns it, no date forces it, and nothing happens if it slips. By the time it surfaces as a problem, the decision that would have prevented it is weeks late.

CLOSE is the discipline that every open item carries an owner, a decision-by date and a stated consequence — so decisions resolve before they cascade.

The belief to drop

The belief is that a well-maintained log is evidence of control — that recording, tracking and reviewing items is the work. So logs grow long and immaculate while the decisions inside them age.

A log records decisions; it does not make them. Control is not the completeness of the list. It is the rate at which items leave it because they were resolved.

Visibility of a problem is not control of it. A risk you can see and cannot close is still a risk.

What CLOSE looks like

Closing decisions means three things attached to every open item, without exception: a named owner who can actually decide, a date by which it must be resolved, and a stated consequence of missing that date. Those three turn a record into a forcing function.

Owner
One accountable name

Not a team, not a function — a person with the authority to decide. Shared ownership is no ownership.

Date
A decision-by date

An open item without a date will drift indefinitely. The date converts “we should sort this” into “this closes by Thursday.”

Consequence
What slips if it does not close

Naming the cost of inaction is what gives the date force. Without a consequence, the date is a suggestion.

How to install it

1
Reject items without all three

An item enters the list only with an owner, a date and a consequence. Items missing any of the three are not framed decisions yet.

2
Review for closure, not for completeness

Run the list to drive items off it, not to confirm it is tidy. The metric is the closure rate, not the record.

3
Escalate on the date, automatically

When a date is about to breach, it escalates by rule, not by someone remembering. The forcing function has to be structural.

The objections

“Some decisions genuinely cannot be made yet.”

Then the open item is “make the decision by date X once input Y arrives,” with an owner for chasing Y. Even a decision that waits has an owner, a date and a consequence. Waiting is not the same as drifting.

“Putting consequences on items feels heavy-handed.”

The consequence is not a threat; it is the truth. Something does slip if the decision is late — naming it simply makes the cost visible before it is paid. Leaving it unnamed does not remove the cost; it just hides it.

AI Reality Check

AI is a tireless closer’s assistant. It can track every open item, watch the decision-by dates, and surface what is about to breach — so nothing sits open merely because everyone forgot it was waiting.

But it can prompt, not own. A decision needs an accountable human with the authority to make it; an AI nudge to an unclear owner just relocates the parking space. Assign the owner, date and consequence first; then let AI guard the dates.

AI can make sure no decision is forgotten. It cannot make sure someone is allowed — and willing — to make it.

What it prevents

An airport infrastructure programme — decisions that closed on time

On a build with dozens of contractor and authority interfaces, the cascades all traced back to the same root: decisions logged and left open — a fire-system sign-off here, an interface detail there — drifting until a downstream trade was blocked on site. Putting an owner, a date and a consequence on every item — and reviewing for closure, not completeness — broke the pattern.

Items began leaving the log because they were resolved, not because they were reworded. The on-site blockages that used to surprise everyone stopped arriving, because the decisions behind them closed on time.

A long, tidy log is not a sign of control.

A short one is — because the items left it the only way that counts: closed, on time, by someone who was allowed to decide.
