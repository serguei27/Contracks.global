A Supplier Date Is Not a Confirmed Date.

Written and narrated by Serguei Poppeleer.

“Yes, we can start Monday.” Three of the most expensive words in delivery — not because the contractor is dishonest, but because they believed it when they said it, and nobody checked whether the supply chain would support them.

A confirmed date is not what a supplier says when asked. It is what their capacity, lead times and readiness will actually support on the day.

The fourth failure mode is the unverified date. A contractor commits based on an optimistic read of their supply chain. The date is entered into the programme as if it were a fact. The float that date consumes is real; the certainty behind it was never there. When the day arrives, the conditions that would have made it true do not exist.

CONFIRM is the discipline of verifying a commitment against evidence before it enters the programme — not accepting it on a promise.

The belief to drop

The belief is that asking a trusted contractor to evidence a date is a sign of distrust — that the relationship does the work verification would otherwise do. So dates are accepted on reputation, and the programme inherits every optimistic estimate as if it were certain.

Verification is not distrust. It is the discipline that protects both sides from a cascade neither intended. The contractors who resist it most are usually the ones whose dates fail most.

Every date accepted without verification is an optimistic promise you have agreed to inherit.

What CONFIRM looks like

Confirming a date means three checks before it enters the programme: capacity confirmed in writing rather than verbally, lead times checked against current conditions rather than past experience, and readiness verified at the source rather than assumed from a dashboard.

Capacity
Written, not verbal

A verbal yes is intent. A written confirmation of quantity, date and terms is a commitment. Only the second belongs in the programme.

Lead time
Current, not remembered

Last year’s three weeks can be this year’s six. Check lead times against current conditions, never against the memory of how it used to go.

Readiness
Verified at the source

Materials still in port, a subcontractor double-booked, a permit pending — readiness is confirmed where the work is, not inferred from a green dashboard.

How to install it

1
Write the verification checklist

Capture the questions your best buyer asks before believing a date. That list becomes the standard anyone can apply.

2
No date enters without evidence

Make written confirmation the default, not an extra step reserved for important projects. The evidence is the price of entry to the programme.

3
Re-verify on a clock

Conditions change; a confirmation from six weeks ago may no longer hold. Set a cadence to re-check live dates against current reality.

The objections

“Asking for written proof damages the supplier relationship.”

A supplier who cannot evidence capacity is handing you a date they cannot guarantee. Asking for evidence is commercial clarity, and the relationship survives a question far better than it survives a missed date built into your programme.

“We have used them for years — we know their lead times.”

You know their past lead times. Supply chains change, and the verification takes twenty minutes; the recovery from a failed commitment takes weeks and the float cannot be replaced. Past performance is context, not confirmation.

AI Reality Check

AI is increasingly strong at the watching. It can monitor supply-chain signals — port backlogs, customs delays, capacity constraints — and flag a committed date drifting weeks before the delivery window closes.

But it verifies against the dates you entered. Point it at a programme built on unconfirmed promises and it will track aspirational dates with great precision. The written confirmation must happen first; then AI keeps it honest over time.

AI can flag that a date is at risk. It cannot have the conversation that should have confirmed it.

What it prevents

A West Africa outstation — verification as a step, not a person

A contractor committed to a delivery date without checking customs clearance from the country of origin. Assumed three weeks; actual six; the programme had been built on the promise, and three weeks of float vanished before the source was found. A pre-commitment verification step — capacity in writing, clearance confirmed with the agent — was introduced.

Across the remaining projects, this class of delay did not recur. The fix was a routine anyone could run, on any project — not a reliance on one person’s instinct for a bluff.

A commitment is what someone hopes will happen.

A confirmed date is what the supply chain will actually support. Only the second one belongs in your programme — and the difference is twenty minutes of verification against weeks of recovery.
