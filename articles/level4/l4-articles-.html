Leading Indicators — Seeing the Slip Before It Shows.

Written and narrated by Serguei Poppeleer.

Every slip is obvious afterwards. The whole skill is seeing it while it’s still avoidable.

Lagging indicators tell you the truth, but always too late: schedule variance, cost overrun, the milestone you watched go past. By the time those numbers move, the slip already happened. You’re measuring history and calling it control.

Leading indicators move first.

They’re quieter, less satisfying to chart, and you have to go looking for them. They’re also the only ones you can still do something about.

A report is a rear-view mirror. Control is looking through the windscreen.

The four signals that move first

1
Decision latency.

How long decisions sit open before someone owns them. The number climbs before any milestone moves — and it climbs in plain sight, if you’re counting.

2
Verified-vs-claimed vendor dates.

How many dates are “confirmed” by email versus actually checked against the vendor’s real capacity. The gap between the two is your delay risk, weeks early.

3
Scope velocity.

How fast scope is changing — and whether anyone is pricing the changes as they land. Unpriced change is a claim forming in slow motion.

4
Plan-to-site distance.

The widening gap between the master plan and what the site actually did this week. It opens quietly, long before the variance report admits it.

None of those show up in a standard monthly report. Every one of them predicts the numbers that eventually will.

A multi-country aviation portfolio — remote delivery

Outstations were run from a single weekly cadence built around leading signals, not status narration. When a supplier date drifted or a decision aged past its window, it surfaced in the cadence — around a week earlier on average than the old reporting would have shown it.

Final accounts closed within contract values. Slippage was caught while it was still cheap to catch. The signals were read before they became the numbers.

The objections this raises

“Leading indicators are soft — the board wants hard numbers.”

Decision latency in days, unverified dates as a count, unpriced change as a value — these are hard numbers. They are simply earlier ones. The board wants no surprises; leading indicators are how you give them that.

“We don’t have the data to track these.”

You generate all of it already — in your decision log, your vendor correspondence, your change register, your plan. The data exists. What’s usually missing is the discipline of reading it as a forecast instead of a record.

AI Reality Check

Leading indicators are tedious to track by hand — which is precisely why they get neglected. AI can watch all four continuously, flagging the decision aging past its date or the email-confirmed vendor that was never verified, the moment the signal moves.

But it watches what you tell it matters. Define the indicators — the 5C inputs — before you automate them. Architecture before automation, every time.

The slip is always visible before it shows — but only if you’re measuring the things that move first.

The line worth keeping

Read properly, the 5Cs are a leading-indicator system. CONFIRM catches the vendor date before it becomes a delay. CLOSE catches the decision before it becomes a bottleneck. CONTROL catches drift before it hardens into variance.

By the time something reaches the report, it is already history.

No-surprises delivery was never luck. It’s reading the leading edge while there’s still time to bend it.
