Prime ICP ·
The PMO Control Lead
Head of PMO / PMO Director · Owner-side · Cross-border CAPEX · Europe ↔ Africa / Gulf · Age 35–55 · Capital-heavy sectors: energy, aviation, real estate, construction, manufacturing, agribusiness · Skeptical of PMO theater.
Ideal Client (ICP)
Who is the ultimate decision maker?
The PMO Control Lead
Accountable for turning messy, high-stakes projects into predictable delivery. Identity tied to bringing order with governance, clear decisions and accountability. Often Europe-based with overseas execution across Africa / Gulf. Insider truth: most organisations have templates but rarely an end-to-end operating system tailored to real country, supply-chain and compliance conditions — so projects look governed but still drift.
Pain — 3 Dominant Points
Hook it · Land it
Hook it
1 · Scope Drift
Land it
Requirements come in half-baked then change mid-stream after supplier quotes land. Weekly steering stuck adjudicating "was that in scope?" 6–12 months later: a portfolio of "mostly complete" projects and a credibility problem with finance because forecasts keep getting revised.
Hook it
2 · Governance Disconnected
Land it
Checklists, stage gates, RAID logs — but no single-page operating plan reflecting site reality. Official plan and real plan diverge fast. Problems surface late, expensive and political because nobody can point to one shared operating truth.
Hook it
3 · Vendor Slippage
Land it
Suppliers miss dates, explanation changes every call. Chasing across email, WhatsApp and spreadsheets. Idle crews, expediting fees, schedule cascades. Business starts treating projects as inherently unreliable.
Deep Fear — Their Core Emotional Driver
I am going to get exposed as a paperwork PMO — good at governance decks, bad at actual control. And when a site or supplier blows the plan up, execs will still pin it on me because I was supposed to be the one running the controls.
Golden insight: They're not scared of complex delivery — scared of being the "control" person without any real levers. Judged on outcomes they cannot directly command. Want control that holds up in the messy middle — decisions, consequences, visibility that survive when suppliers and sites push back.
Signature Method — Your Essential Principles / Pillars
The 5Cs
The Delivery Control System™
1
Contain
the Scope
Turn fuzzy requirements into one written scope — clear assumptions, acceptance criteria and what is explicitly out of scope.
2
Command
One Plan
Replace deck governance with one operating plan that matches how site/production actually runs week to week.
3
Close
Every Decision
Set decision owners, deadlines and consequences so open items get closed before they become delays.
4
Confirm
Vendor Dates
Rebuild supplier commitments around capacity, lead times, handoffs and proof of readiness — not optimistic promises.
5
Control
Tower Cadence
Fixed weekly rhythm — same inputs, same meeting, same outputs — so slippage is caught early and corrected fast.
Mistakes — What's really causing the pain?
Wrong thinking → Wrong action
Wrong thinking
"We can start now and tighten scope later — everyone roughly knows what we mean."
Wrong action
→ Kicking off from a slide deck without a written scope — debating "was that included?" in every meeting.
Wrong thinking
"As long as stage gates look right, delivery will follow."
Wrong action
→ Polished template updated for governance while site runs off WhatsApp — plans diverge by week 2.
Wrong thinking
"I can't force a decision — I just need to keep everyone aligned."
Wrong action
→ RAID logs with no owner, no deadline, no consequence — scramble when they cascade.
Wrong thinking
"Procurement got the commitment — if they miss, it's on them."
Wrong action
→ Accepting supplier dates without verifying capacity, lead times and readiness.
Wrong thinking
"If we meet often enough and share updates, we'll stay on top."
Wrong action
→ Status calls with no fixed inputs, outputs or follow-through — slippage discovered late, not early.
Payoff — 3 Big Benefits
Desired + without + resolution
Hook it
1 · Predictable Delivery
Desire
I can tell the execs — with confidence — what is on track, what is off track, and exactly what we're doing about it before it becomes a fire.
Without
Adding a second reporting layer (deck factory) or RAG status that hides real risk.
Resolution
Controls feel real in the work — delivery becomes steady instead of surprise-driven.
Hook it
2 · Fast Decisions
Desire
When something shifts — site, supplier, design — decisions get made in days not weeks, with a clear owner, clear options and clear trade-offs.
Without
Endless steering meetings, vague actions or decision logs no one follows.
Resolution
The project moves forward because decisions are forced to conclusion — not deferred until the plan breaks.
Hook it
3 · Real Control
Desire
If a key milestone slips, I can show cause → impact → recovery plan, and the team actually executes it — not just agrees in a meeting.
Without
Overcorrecting into micromanagement or pretending a recovery plan exists when it's just hopeful actions.
Resolution
The PMO is seen as the control tower that keeps delivery stable — not a paperwork function that gets blamed when reality hits.
