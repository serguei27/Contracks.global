Delivery Control Readiness

How close are you to No-Surprises Delivery?

21 questions, about four minutes. Answer for the programme you're closest to. No score is shown to anyone but you.

Contain

Scope — is it defined, and does it hold?
The full scope is written down — with assumptions and what's explicitly out of scope.
When scope changes, it's priced and re-baselined before work proceeds — not absorbed and argued later.
Everyone accountable would point to the same definition of "done" for the current phase.

Command

One plan — does reported match real?
There is one operating plan that matches how the work actually runs week to week — not a steering deck that has drifted from reality.
You'd be comfortable if the board saw the working plan unedited, because it's the same as the reported one.
Cross-contractor and cross-border interfaces have a single owner and a single source of truth.

Close

Decisions — do they get forced to a conclusion?
Every open decision has a named owner, a deadline, and a stated consequence if it slips.
Decisions are forced to a conclusion — they don't sit in a RAID log waiting to become a delay.
You can see today which decisions are overdue and exactly what they're blocking.

Confirm

Vendor dates — verified, or taken on trust?
Vendor and contractor commitments are verified against capacity, lead times and readiness — not accepted on promise.
Critical handoffs between suppliers are mapped and confirmed, not assumed.
When a date is given, there is defined proof of readiness behind it.

Control

Cadence — does the system catch slippage early?
There is a fixed control cadence — same inputs, same meeting, same outputs, every cycle.
Slippage is caught early in the cadence, not discovered late in a report.
For any issue, the programme can show a clear cause → impact → recovery chain on demand.

The bottom line

The one question the others build toward.
If a serious surprise were forming right now, you're confident your controls would surface it before it reached the board.
